# Marbury v. Madison: Judicial Review Explained

**Date:** November 25, 2025  
**Category:** Case Brief  
**Keywords:** Constitutional Law, Judicial Review, Supreme Court

---

## Overview

*Marbury v. Madison*, 5 U.S. (1 Cranch) 137 (1803), stands as perhaps the most significant Supreme Court decision in American legal history. Chief Justice John Marshall's landmark opinion not only resolved a politically charged dispute but established the enduring principle of **judicial review**—the power of courts to declare laws unconstitutional. This case brief examines the facts, procedural posture, constitutional issues, and lasting impact of this foundational decision.

---

## Facts

In the waning days of President John Adams's administration in March 1801, Congress expanded the federal judiciary, and Adams appointed several judges and justices of the peace to fill new positions. William Marbury was nominated and confirmed as Justice of the Peace for the District of Columbia. 

As part of the appointment process, Secretary of State John Marshall (who would soon become Chief Justice) prepared Marbury's commission. However, before the commission could be delivered, Adams's term ended and Thomas Jefferson became president. Jefferson, viewing the appointments as a political maneuver by his Federalist predecessor, instructed his new Secretary of State, James Madison, to withhold delivery of Marbury's commission.

Marbury, denied his office, sought a remedy directly in the Supreme Court, filing a suit for a writ of mandamus to compel Madison to deliver the commission.

---

## Procedural Posture

Marbury sued James Madison directly in the Supreme Court, invoking the Court's **original jurisdiction** under Section 13 of the Judiciary Act of 1789. He argued that the writ of mandamus—a court order commanding a public official to perform his legal duty—was the appropriate remedy to compel delivery of his commission.

The Supreme Court thus faced the question of whether it had the power to issue such a writ in this original action.

---

## Issues

1. **Substantive Issue:** Did Marbury have a vested right to his commission?

2. **Procedural Issue:** Does the Supreme Court have jurisdiction to issue a writ of mandamus in this original action under Section 13 of the Judiciary Act of 1789?

3. **Constitutional Issue:** Can Congress constitutionally expand the Supreme Court's original jurisdiction beyond what Article III of the Constitution specifies?

---

## The Court's Holding

Chief Justice Marshall held:

1. **On the Merits:** Marbury did have a vested right to his commission once it was signed and sealed by the President. The withholding by Secretary of State Madison was a violation of Marbury's rights.

2. **On Jurisdiction:** However, the Supreme Court lacked jurisdiction to issue the mandamus because Section 13 of the Judiciary Act, which granted the Court this power in original actions, was **unconstitutional**.

3. **The Central Holding:** The Supreme Court possesses the power of **judicial review**—the authority to review acts of Congress and other branches of government for constitutionality and to strike down those that violate the Constitution.

---

## Ratio Decidendi (The Legal Reasoning)

### The Supremacy of the Constitution

Marshall began with a fundamental principle: the Constitution is the supreme law of the land. If the Constitution and a statute conflict, the Constitution must prevail. Courts have a duty to uphold the Constitution and cannot enforce unconstitutional laws.

### The Role of the Judiciary

Marshall argued that it is "emphatically the province and duty of the judicial department to say what the law is." When courts are asked to interpret statutes or constitutional provisions, they must determine whether legislative acts are consistent with the Constitution.

### Article III Limitation

Marshall examined Article III of the Constitution, which specifies the Supreme Court's original jurisdiction:

> "The judicial power of the United States shall be vested in one supreme Court, and in such inferior Courts as the Congress may from time to time ordain and establish... In all Cases affecting Ambassadors, other public Ministers and Consuls, and those in which a State shall be a Party, the supreme Court shall have original Jurisdiction. In all the other Cases before mentioned, the supreme Court shall have appellate Jurisdiction."

Section 13 of the Judiciary Act attempted to grant the Court original jurisdiction in mandamus cases. Marshall concluded that this statute attempted to add to the Court's original jurisdiction beyond what the Constitution permitted.

### The Unconstitutionality Finding

Since Section 13 conflicted with the Constitution's explicit delineation of the Court's original jurisdiction, it was unconstitutional and void. The Court therefore lacked jurisdiction over Marbury's original action for mandamus.

### The Strategic Outcome

By ruling the statute unconstitutional, Marshall solved the political problem: he did not have to order Madison to deliver the commission (which would have been ignored by Jefferson), yet the Court established its power to strike down unconstitutional acts.

---

## Significance and Legacy

### Judicial Review

*Marbury* established the foundational principle that courts may review the constitutionality of legislative and executive acts. This remains central to American constitutional law and the power of courts to check governmental action.

### Separation of Powers

The decision reinforced separation of powers by establishing that each branch must respect constitutional limits on its authority. No branch—including Congress—can exceed its constitutional powers.

### Constitutional Interpretation

Marshall's reasoning established that the Constitution is not merely political rhetoric but judicially enforceable law. Constitutional provisions limit governmental action in concrete, legally cognizable ways.

### Federalism

By striking down a federal statute, *Marbury* demonstrated that even laws passed by Congress could be invalidated, creating a powerful check on federal power.

---

## Critical Analysis

### Strengths of Marshall's Opinion

- **Logical Framework:** The opinion presents a compelling logical structure: the Constitution is supreme; courts must interpret law; the statute conflicts with the Constitution; therefore, the statute is void.
- **Judicial Role:** Marshall persuasively articulated the judicial function in interpreting and applying law.
- **Political Brilliance:** By ruling against the statute rather than forcing a confrontation, Marshall avoided a constitutional crisis while establishing the Court's power.

### Criticisms and Debates

- **Textual Authority:** Some scholars argue that the Constitution does not explicitly grant courts the power to invalidate statutes.
- **Judicial Restraint:** Critics contend that judicial review is countermajoritarian and conflicts with democratic principles.
- **Marshall's Logic:** The reasoning that the Constitution's explicit grant of original jurisdiction means Congress cannot add to it has been questioned, as later legislation expanded federal jurisdiction.

---

## Impact on American Law

*Marbury v. Madison* stands as the foundation for:

- Judicial review of all governmental action
- Constitutional supremacy
- The judiciary as a coequal branch of government
- The power of the courts to enforce constitutional limits
- Modern civil rights protections (grounded in judicial enforcement of constitutional guarantees)

---

## Conclusion

*Marbury v. Madison* remains one of the most important cases in American law not because it resolved a great national emergency, but because it quietly established the principle that would define the American constitutional system: the courts have the power and duty to say what the law is, and to ensure that all governmental action complies with the supreme law of the Constitution. Through elegant reasoning and strategic restraint, Chief Justice Marshall transformed a political embarrassment into constitutional doctrine that has shaped American law for over two centuries.
