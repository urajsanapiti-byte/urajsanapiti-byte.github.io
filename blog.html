The Protection of Women from Domestic Violence Act, 2005

Introduction The Protection of Women from Domestic Violence Act, 2005 (PWDVA), stands as a legislative milestone in India's ongoing response to pervasive domestic violence against women. Defined under Section 3 of the Act, domestic violence encompasses not just acts of physical and sexual harm, but also extends to emotional and economic abuse, mental harassment, and threats to a woman’s health or safety, as well as unlawful demands such as dowry. (National Crime Records Bureau. (2022). Crime in India: 2022 Statistics.) It was brought into force by the Indian government from 26 October 2006.The act relates strongly to gender inclusion by recognizing various forms of abuse, providing women in diverse domestic relationships access to immediate civil remedies and protection. Over the past two decades, the Act’s implementation has empowered many women by providing immediate protection orders, residence rights, and monetary relief without waiting for lengthy criminal proceedings. From the perspective of a law, I recognize the Act as a vital instrument that promotes gender inclusion by acknowledging domestic violence’s multifaceted nature and by creating a supportive framework that addresses survivors' civil rights alongside criminal remedies

Scope and Coverage of the Act Recent data reveals that the experience of domestic violence is both widespread and deeply entrenched. As per the National Family Health Survey (NFHS-5, 2019-2021), 29.3% of ever-married women aged 18-49 reported having experienced spousal violence, a slight reduction from the 31.2% reported in the previous survey cycle (2015–2016). Geographic variability is stark: states like Manipur reported rates as high as 55%, while Sikkim and Himachal Pradesh had incidences as low as 4% and 7%, respectively. Notably, nearly 87% of women who faced marital violence did not seek any form of help, citing social stigma, fear of reprisal, and lack of awareness as major barriers. (Bisma Sabri et al., Violence Against Women in India: A Systematic Review of Risk and Protective Factors, 19 Int’l J. Envtl. Rsch. & Pub. Health 9756932 (2022).

Objectives • The Act was formulated to provide immediate and effective protection to women facing various forms of domestic violence, recognizing abuse not only as physical harm but also emotional, verbal, sexual, and economic in nature. • The Act promotes gender inclusion by extending protection to women in diverse domestic relationships, including married, live-in, and blood relatives, highlighting attention to the varied realities of women's lives. • A key objective is to offer speedy civil remedies such as protection orders, monetary relief, custody arrangements, and compensation, ensuring women’s safety and dignity are restored without solely relying on criminal proceedings. • The Act encourages changing societal attitudes towards domestic violence by raising awareness and empowering women to assert their rights, progressing from private family issues to recognized legal and social concerns.

Challenges Major issue is the slow and uneven implementation across states. For instance, Maharashtra appointed 3,730 Protection Officers, while Assam and West Bengal had fewer than 25 each, demonstrating serious regional disparities in personnel allocation and support infrastructure. Additionally, the conviction rate under the Act is low, with only 19.1% of cases resulting in conviction as of 2014, and many cases face significant delays in judicial processes, undermining timely justice for survivors (Das & Lakshmana, 2020). The Supreme Court of India has recently issued directions mandating the appointment of dedicated Protection Officers from the Department of Women and Child Development at district levels to improve enforcement and victim support (Supreme Court, 2025).

Analysis Its gender-specific framework explicitly defines an "aggrieved person" as any woman in a domestic relationship and a "respondent" as any adult male person in such a relationship, thereby structurally excluding male victims, LGBTQ+ individuals, and non-heteronormative families from its protections. This gendered legal definition aligns with the Constitution’s goal to protect women’s rights but also creates critical gaps in inclusivity and coverage (Gupta & Wamankar, 2025) The prevailing opinion among scholars advocates for expanding the Act’s definitional scope to include all victims irrespective of gender, while maintaining the emphasis on protecting women, who statistically suffer disproportionately more domestic violence. Introduction to Contract Law Principles

Contract law forms the backbone of commercial transactions and private relationships. This introduction explores the fundamental principles that govern the formation, interpretation, and enforcement of contracts. Whether you're entering into a business agreement or understanding everyday transactions, knowledge of these core concepts is essential to navigating the legal landscape.

What is a Contract? A contract is a legally binding agreement between two or more parties. For a contract to be enforceable, it must contain several essential elements: offer, acceptance, consideration, intent to be bound, certainty of terms, and compliance with any applicable formalities.

Offer and Acceptance: An offer is a manifestation of willingness to enter into a bargain, made so as to justify another person in understanding that his assent to that bargain is invited and will conclude it. Acceptance is an expression of agreement to the terms of the offer. Together, offer and acceptance form the consensus ad idem (meeting of the minds) necessary for contract formation.

Consideration: Consideration is the value exchanged by the parties. It can be money, services, goods, or a promise. Both parties must provide consideration for the contract to be binding. A unilateral promise without consideration is generally not binding.

Intent to Be Bound: The parties must intend to create legal relations. In commercial contexts, this intent is presumed; in social or domestic contexts, it must be clearly demonstrated.

Certainty of Terms: The terms of the contract must be sufficiently certain to be enforceable. If essential terms are left vague or indefinite, the contract may be unenforceable for uncertainty.
